Constitution — The Basics

Foundation facts every BPSC aspirant must know
⚠️

BPSC Exam Tip

Basic facts like "World's longest written constitution", number of articles, schedules, and parts are directly asked as MCQs in Prelims almost every year.
📖

World's Longest Written Constitution

Originally 395 articles in 22 parts & 8 schedules. Now 448 articles, 25 parts, 12 schedules.

⚖️

Federal with Unitary Bias

India has a federal structure but tilts toward the Centre during emergencies — called "Quasi-Federal."

🗳️

Parliamentary System

Borrowed from UK. Executive is accountable to the legislature. PM is the real head of government.

📝

Written & Enacted

It is a written constitution unlike UK's unwritten one. Enacted by a Constituent Assembly — not parliament.

🔒

Partly Rigid, Partly Flexible

Some articles need special majority + state ratification. Others need only simple majority.

🛡️

Fundamental Rights Guaranteed

6 Fundamental Rights enshrined in Part III (Articles 12–35). Enforceable in court.

The Preamble — Soul of the Constitution

Most frequently asked topic in BPSC Prelims & Mains

✦ The Preamble of India ✦

"WE, THE PEOPLE OF INDIA, having solemnly resolved to constitute India into a SOVEREIGN SOCIALIST SECULAR DEMOCRATIC REPUBLIC and to secure to all its citizens: JUSTICE, Social, Economic and Political; LIBERTY of thought, expression, belief, faith and worship; EQUALITY of status and opportunity; and to promote among them all FRATERNITY assuring the dignity of the individual and the unity and integrity of the Nation; IN OUR CONSTITUENT ASSEMBLY this twenty-sixth day of November, 1949, do hereby ADOPT, ENACT AND GIVE TO OURSELVES THIS CONSTITUTION."

🔑 5 Keywords of the Preamble Explained

🏴
SOVEREIGN
India is fully independent — not under control of any foreign power. Free in both internal & external affairs.
⚖️
SOCIALIST
Mixed economy — both public & private sectors. Aims for equitable distribution of wealth. Added by 42nd Amendment, 1976.
🕌
SECULAR
No state religion. All religions get equal respect & support. Added by 42nd Amendment, 1976.
🗳️
DEMOCRATIC
People elect their representatives through free & fair elections. Constitution derives authority from citizens.
🏛️
REPUBLIC
Head of state (President) is elected — not hereditary. No hereditary monarchy in India.

⚖️

🕊️
LIBERTY

🤝

🫂

⚖️ Landmark Cases on the Preamble

Case Year Ruling
Berubari Case 1960 Preamble is NOT a part of Constitution. Cannot be enforced in court.
Kesavananda Bharati Case 1973 Preamble IS part of Constitution. Forms part of Basic Structure — cannot be destroyed.
LIC of India Case 1995 Reaffirmed: Preamble is integral to the Constitution. Not directly enforceable.

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar submitted the draft Constitution to the Constituent Assembly. It took 2 years, 11 months and 18 days to complete.

The Constitution was adopted by the Constituent Assembly. This day is celebrated as Constitution Day (Samvidhan Divas) since 2015. The Preamble was adopted on this day.

📌 Most Frequently Asked Date
26 Jan 1950

Constitution came into force. India became a Republic. 26 January was chosen because it was the anniversary of "Purna Swaraj" Declaration (1930) by the INC.

Supreme Court (8-judge bench) ruled Preamble is NOT part of Constitution.

📌 Asked in BPSC Mains

13-judge bench ruled Preamble IS part of Constitution and part of Basic Structure. Parliament cannot destroy it.

📌 Very Frequently Asked in BPSC Mains

The 42nd Constitutional Amendment added the words 'Socialist' and 'Secular' to the Preamble. Also changed "Unity of the Nation" to "Unity and Integrity of the Nation".

⚖️ Five Writs under Article 32 & 226

Habeas Corpus
"Produce the body" — Protection of individual liberty against illegal detention.
Mandamus
"We command" — Directs public authority to perform a duty it is bound to perform.
Prohibition
Prevents lower courts from exceeding their jurisdiction.
Certiorari
Quashes order of lower court that exceeded jurisdiction or made error of law.
Quo Warranto
"By what authority?" — Challenges unlawful occupation of public office.

Important Questions & Answers — Last 20 Years (1999–2023)

14 BPSC Mains & Prelims questions with trend analysis predicting BPSC 2026 exam pattern

Trend Analysis Included

This section includes frequency analysis, predicted topics for 2026, and current affairs integration for maximum exam relevance.

📝 Topic 1: Preamble & Constitution Basics (⭐⭐⭐ VERY HIGH)

Q1 (BPSC Mains 1999) ⭐⭐⭐
Examine the relevance and significance of the philosophy enshrined in the Preamble of the Constitution of India.
Answer: The Preamble defines objectives through: (1) Justice (Social, Economic, Political); (2) Equality; (3) Liberty; (4) Fraternity. Establishes India as Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic Republic. Non-enforceable initially but now guides interpretation (Kesavananda Bharati 1973).
Q2 (BPSC Mains 2002) ⭐⭐⭐
Evaluate the nature and significance of the Preamble of the Indian Constitution.
Answer: Nature: Introductory, aspirational, interpretive aid. Significance: Moral foundation, democratic legitimacy, allows evolution (42nd Amendment added Socialist, Secular).
Q3 (BPSC Mains 2019) ⭐⭐⭐
"The Indian Constitution postulates in its Preamble that India shall be a socialist, secular, democratic republic." What are the constitutional provisions to enforce this?
Answer: Socialist: Art 39, 43 | Secular: Art 14-18, 25-28 | Democratic: Art 326, Part IX&X | Republic: Art 52-150
Q4 (BPSC Mains 2023) ⭐⭐⭐
Explain how the Preamble of the Indian Constitution provides a blueprint about the goals of the Constitution.
Answer: Preamble as blueprint: Structural goals (Sovereign, Democratic, Republic) + Substantive goals (JELF). All laws, policies, court decisions must align. Courts use to interpret ambiguities.

📝 Topic 2: Fundamental Rights & Article 21 (⭐⭐⭐ VERY HIGH)

Q5 (BPSC Mains 1993) ⭐⭐⭐
Describe the Fundamental Duties as enumerated in the Constitution of India.
Answer: 11 Fundamental Duties (Article 51A), added by 42nd Amendment (1976), non-justiciable. Includes: respect Constitution, defend country, promote fraternity, protect environment (11th, 86th Amendment 2002). Moral obligations for responsible citizenship.
Q6 (BPSC Mains 2001) ⭐⭐⭐
Make a critical evaluation of the right to property as it exists today.
Answer: Downgraded from Fundamental (1950) → Constitutional (1978, 44th Amendment) → Art 300-A. State can acquire for public purpose with compensation. Criticism: Weakens investment security. Reform: 2013 Land Acquisition Bill requires stricter consent.
Q7 (BPSC Mains 2016) ⭐⭐⭐
How has judicial interpretation of Article 21 expanded the scope of Right to Life?
Answer: Article 21 expanded from arrest protection to: Education (Unni Krishnan 1993), Privacy (Puttaswamy 2017), Dignified death (P. Rathinam 1994), Health, Shelter, Livelihood, Environment. Landmark: Maneka Gandhi (1978) established substantive rights.
Q8 (BPSC Mains 2020) ⭐⭐⭐
Do you agree that the Constitution has given Fundamental Rights by one hand and taken them back?
Answer: PARTIALLY TRUE. Rights have reasonable restrictions (Art 19-2, 15-4) for national security, public order. This is "balancing" not "takeback." Verdict: Constitution balances individual freedoms with collective welfare—a feature, not a flaw.

📝 Topic 3: Amendment Procedure & Judicial Review (⭐⭐⭐ VERY HIGH)

Q9 (BPSC Mains 1995) ⭐⭐⭐
Is Indian Parliament Sovereign or non-sovereign?
Answer: CONSTITUTIONALLY LIMITED NOT SOVEREIGN. Cannot violate Fundamental Rights (Art 13), alter basic structure (Kesavananda Bharati), subject to judicial review. Verdict: Legally sovereign but constitutionally limited = responsible democracy.
Q10 (BPSC Mains 2002) ⭐⭐⭐
What is the amendment procedure in India?
Answer: Three types: (1) Simple majority, (2) Special majority (2/3 both houses), (3) Ratified (2/3 + 1/2 states). Original (1950): 395 articles → Current (2026): 448 articles, 105+ amendments. Key: 42nd (1976-Socialist), 73rd (1992-Panchayati), 86th (2002-Education).
Q11 (BPSC Mains 2019) ⭐⭐⭐
Critically examine the doctrine of basic structure.
Answer: Kesavananda Bharati (1973): Cannot amend indivisible elements (Sovereignty, Democratic nature, Federalism, Secularism, FR, Separation of Powers) even by special majority. Protects Constitution from dictatorship BUT raises judicial overreach concerns.
Q12 (BPSC Mains 2020) ⭐⭐⭐
"Indian Parliament is a non-sovereign law making institute." Evaluate.
Answer: AGREE: Cannot make laws violating FR or basic structure, subject to judicial review. DISAGREE: Parliament IS legally sovereign in legislative domain, CAN amend Constitution itself (Art 368). BALANCED: Parliament is sovereign but constitutionally limited.
Q13 (BPSC Mains 2023) ⭐⭐⭐
Critically describe the amendment process of the Indian Constitution.
Answer: Process: Introduction → First Reading → Second Reading (clause-by-clause) → Passage → Presidential Assent → Publication. Strengths: Flexibility, safeguards. Weaknesses: Can be misused (Emergency), concentrates power, conflicts with basic structure.

📝 Topic 4: Writs & Constitutional Remedies (Article 32) (⭐⭐ HIGH)

Q14 (Implicit from BPSC Pattern) ⭐⭐
Explain the five writs under Article 32 and 226.
Answer: Dr. Ambedkar called Article 32 "Heart & Soul" of Constitution:
  • Habeas Corpus: "Produce body" → Release from illegal detention
  • Mandamus: "We command" → Direct public official to perform duty
  • Prohibition: "We forbid" → Prevent lower courts exceeding jurisdiction
  • Certiorari: "To be certified" → Quash unlawful lower court orders
  • Quo Warranto: "By what authority" → Challenge unlawful office occupation

Q1 (BPSC Mains 1999)

Answer: The Preamble defines the objectives of the Indian Constitution through four key aspects:
  • Objectives: Justice (Social, Economic, Political), Equality, and Liberty
  • Nature of State: Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic Republic
  • Binding Force: Though not enforceable initially, it now guides constitutional interpretation (Kesavananda Bharati 1973)
  • Fraternity: Maintains unity and national integration

Evaluate the nature and significance of the Preamble of the Indian Constitution.

  • Nature: Introductory document listing constitutional objectives; aspirational in tone ("We, the people..."); interpretive aid for courts

  • Socialist: Articles 39 (DPSP for equal resources), 43 (right to work & fair wages)
  • Secular: Articles 14-18 (equality), 25-28 (freedom of religion)
  • Democratic: Article 326 (universal adult suffrage), Part IX & X (local governance)
  • Republic: Articles 52-150 (elective head of state)

📝 Topic 2: Fundamental Rights & Article 21

Q4 (BPSC Mains 2016)
Discuss about Fundamental Rights. How has judicial interpretation of Article 21 expanded the scope of Right to Life?
Answer: Article 21 has expanded from mere freedom from arrest to comprehensive protection:
  • ✅ Right to education (Unni Krishnan 1993)
  • ✅ Right to privacy (Justice K.S. Puttaswamy 2017)
  • ✅ Right to dignified death (P. Rathinam 1994)
  • ✅ Right to shelter & health (Olga Tellis 1986)
  • ✅ Right to clean environment (M.C. Mehta 1992)
  • ✅ Right to livelihood (Mithu v. State 1983)
Landmark: Maneka Gandhi (1978) established that Article 21 includes substantive rights, not just procedural protection.
Q5 (BPSC Mains 2020)
Do you agree that the Constitution has given Fundamental Rights by one hand and taken them back by another hand?
Answer: PARTIALLY TRUE — The Constitution grants rights but allows "reasonable" restrictions:
  • ✅ Article 19 grants speech but allows restrictions for national security, decency, defamation
  • ✅ Article 14 grants equality but permits Article 15(4) reservations for backward classes
  • ✅ Article 21 grants right to life but permits capital punishment
  • ❌ Restrictions are NOT arbitrary takeback but "reasonable balancing" for public good
Conclusion: Constitution balances individual freedoms with collective welfare — a feature, not a flaw.

📝 Topic 3: Amendment Procedure & Basic Structure

Q6 (BPSC Mains 2019)
What is judicial review? Critically examine the doctrine of basic structure as enunciated by the Supreme Court.
Answer:
  • Judicial Review: Court's power to examine if laws/amendments comply with Constitution
  • Basic Structure Doctrine (Kesavananda Bharati 1973): Cannot amend essential constitutional features even by special majority
  • Indivisible Elements: Sovereignty, Democratic nature, Federalism, Secularism, Rule of Law, Fundamental Rights, Separation of Powers
  • Debate: Protects Constitution from dictatorship BUT limits democratic amendments
Q7 (BPSC Mains 1995)
What is the meaning of "Parliamentary Sovereignty"? Is Indian Parliament Sovereign or non-sovereign?
Answer: Indian Parliament is CONSTITUTIONALLY LIMITED, NOT SOVEREIGN
  • ✅ Cannot violate Fundamental Rights (Article 13)
  • ✅ Cannot alter basic structure (Kesavananda Bharati)
  • ✅ Bound by Constitutional limits (Articles 245-248)
  • ✅ Subject to judicial review by Supreme Court
  • ✅ Cannot legislate on State List subjects without concurrent power
Conclusion: Legally sovereign BUT constitutionally limited = responsible democracy.
